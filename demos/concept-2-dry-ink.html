Cloudflare's Rule-Breaking Quarter and What It Means for the Portfolio

Drew · March 28, 2026

There's a particular kind of earnings report that makes you sit up straighter. Not the ones that beat estimates by a penny and guide in-line. The ones that break the pattern. Cloudflare's Q4 was that kind of quarter.

Revenue came in at $498 million, up 29% year-over-year. That's an acceleration from 28% the prior quarter. At a $2 billion run rate, that's not supposed to happen. The law of large numbers is supposed to be pulling growth down, not pushing it up. And yet here we are.

When a company accelerates revenue growth at this scale, the market hasn't priced it in. It can't — because most models assume deceleration is inevitable.

The driver was Workers AI and the broader AI gateway story. Enterprise customers are routing inference traffic through Cloudflare's network, and the usage-based revenue is compounding on top of the subscription base. CEO Matthew Prince called it "the fastest-growing product in company history," and for once, that kind of CEO superlative was backed up by the numbers.

What the Saul Rules Say

Cloudflare passes 9 of 11 rules cleanly. Revenue growth above 25% at scale, check. Net dollar retention above 120%, check. The two soft areas — profitability trajectory and insider selling — are both improving. Operating margin turned positive this quarter for the first time on a GAAP basis.

The position is now 11.2% of the portfolio. I'm comfortable with that weighting. When a company is executing this well, the risk isn't in holding too much — it's in holding too little because you were anchored to a lower price.

The Uncomfortable Part

Valuation. At 22x forward revenue, this is not cheap by any historical standard. But "cheap" is a relative concept in growth investing. A company growing 29% with expanding margins and a massive TAM in front of it is a different animal than a company growing 29% because of a one-time COVID pull-forward.

I've been wrong before about paying up for quality. I've also been wrong about selling too early because a stock "looked expensive." The second mistake has cost me more money over the years. So the position stays.
